Alpha & Beta: What They Really Mean

Every private banker will talk about generating alpha. Here’s what it actually means — and why confusing it with beta is one of the most expensive mistakes a new investor makes.

The Two Numbers Behind Every Investment Pitch

When your wealth manager presents returns, they will often mention alpha and beta. These two Greek letters describe fundamentally different things — and conflating them is how fees get hidden in plain sight.

Beta: the return the market gives you for free

Beta measures how much an investment moves relative to its benchmark. A portfolio with a beta of 1.0 moves in lockstep with its index. A beta of 1.2 means it rises 12% when the index rises 10% — and falls 12% when the index falls 10%. A beta of 0.7 means it absorbs only 70% of market swings.

Beta is not skill. It is market exposure. Any investor can get beta cheaply — a simple index fund tracking the S&P 500 or MSCI World costs as little as 0.03–0.07% per year in fees.

Alpha: the return that only skill can produce

Alpha is the return above and beyond what beta explains. If your portfolio returned 12% in a year when the market returned 10%, and your portfolio has a beta of 1.0, your manager generated approximately 2% of alpha.

“How much of my return is alpha, and how much is just beta I could get more cheaply elsewhere?”

Why this matters for inherited wealth

Inherited portfolios often arrive with a legacy of active management relationships built over decades. Some generate genuine alpha — most do not. Research consistently shows that over 80% of active fund managers underperform their benchmark after fees over 10-year periods (S&P SPIVA Scorecard, 2024).

Your job as a new steward is not to assume that yesterday’s managers are tomorrow’s alpha generators. It is to require evidence.

What to ask your banker
  1. “What is this portfolio’s beta to its benchmark?”
  2. “What is the alpha, net of all fees, over the last 5 and 10 years?”
  3. “How much of our portfolio am I paying active management fees for beta I could get passively?”
The formula (simplified)
Portfolio Return = (Beta × Market Return) + Alpha + Fees

The goal of a well-structured family office is to maximise alpha while being intentional — not accidental — about how much beta exposure you carry.

The Sharpe Ratio: Risk-Adjusted Returns

Two funds both returned 12% last year. They are not equal. The Sharpe ratio tells you which one took a reckless amount of risk to get there.

Comparing Returns Properly

Raw returns are almost meaningless without context. A 15% return achieved by betting on a single volatile asset is very different from a 12% return produced by a well-diversified portfolio with low volatility.

The formula
Sharpe Ratio = (Portfolio Return − Risk-Free Rate) ÷ Standard Deviation of Returns
  • Portfolio Return: what your portfolio actually earned
  • Risk-Free Rate: typically the yield on short-term government bonds — the “free” return you could have earned with no risk
  • Standard Deviation: how wildly the portfolio’s returns fluctuated month to month
What a good Sharpe ratio looks like
Sharpe RatioInterpretation
Below 0Return did not beat the risk-free rate — you took risk for nothing
0 – 0.5Adequate, but not impressive
0.5 – 1.0Good — reasonable return per unit of risk
Above 1.0Excellent — strong risk-adjusted performance
Above 2.0Exceptional — rare outside top-quartile managers
Why it matters for heirs

When comparing investment managers, always ask for Sharpe ratios over 3 and 10 year periods, not just raw returns. A manager who delivered 18% returns but with a Sharpe of 0.3 took enormous risk to get there. A manager with 11% returns and a Sharpe of 1.2 almost certainly served their clients better.

The Sortino improvement

The Sortino ratio is the Sharpe ratio’s more sophisticated cousin. It only penalises downside volatility (losses), not upside volatility (gains). Since heirs are typically more concerned with protecting capital than with upside swings, the Sortino is often more relevant for your purposes.

Portfolio Construction 101

How do the world’s most sophisticated investors actually build a portfolio? It starts with an Investment Policy Statement and ends with disciplined rebalancing — everything in between is where most heirs make mistakes.

From Scattered Assets to a Coherent Portfolio

Many inherited portfolios are not portfolios at all — they are collections of historical decisions: a property bought in 2001, a hedge fund relationship from 2008, a stock position that was never sold. Your first task as a new steward is to understand what you actually own — and then build toward intention.

Step 1: The Investment Policy Statement (IPS)

Before buying or selling anything, a family office should have an Investment Policy Statement. This is a written document that defines:

  • Return objective: what annualised real return you need to meet your spending goals
  • Risk tolerance: the maximum drawdown (peak-to-trough loss) you can emotionally and practically sustain
  • Time horizon: are you investing for your generation, or in perpetuity?
  • Liquidity requirements: how much cash must be accessible within 90 days?
  • Constraints: ethical restrictions, concentration limits, currency exposure limits
  • Asset allocation targets: the strategic mix across asset classes

Without an IPS, you have no framework for saying no to opportunities — and every manager will pitch you an “exceptional opportunity.”

Step 2: Strategic Asset Allocation

Strategic allocation is the long-term target mix across broad asset classes. It is the single most important determinant of your portfolio’s long-term returns — more important than any individual stock pick or manager selection.

Step 3: Diversification — the only free lunch

Nobel laureate Harry Markowitz called diversification the only free lunch in investing. By combining assets with low or negative correlation, you can reduce overall portfolio risk without proportionally reducing return.

Correlation matters more than most heirs realise. During the 2022 market downturn, both equities and bonds fell simultaneously — a reminder that the traditional 60/40 model assumes low bond-equity correlation, which does not always hold.

Step 4: Manager Selection

Within each asset class, you need to decide: active or passive management? For public equity markets — especially liquid, efficient markets like large-cap US equities — passive index funds typically outperform active managers after fees. For less efficient markets (emerging markets, small caps, private equity, private credit), skilled active managers can add genuine alpha.

Step 5: Rebalancing

Markets drift. A 40% equity allocation becomes 50% after a bull run. Rebalancing — selling what has grown and buying what has lagged — enforces discipline and systematically buys low, sells high. Most family offices rebalance annually or when any asset class drifts more than 5% from its target.

The Yale Endowment Model

Yale’s endowment has returned 9.4% per annum over the last decade — outperforming a simple 70/30 stock-bond portfolio by 2.2% per year. Here’s the philosophy behind one of the most influential investment models ever built.

Pioneering the Alternatives Era

The Yale Endowment — now valued at $44.1 billion — is one of the most studied investment portfolios in the world. Managed by Yale’s Investment Office under the philosophy pioneered by the late David Swensen (CIO 1985–2021) and continued by current CIO Matt Mendelsohn, it has produced a 10-year annualised return of 9.4% (as of June 2025), exceeding the median peer endowment by 1.4% per annum and outperforming a 70/30 stock-bond portfolio by 2.2% per year.

The core principles of the Yale Model

1. Equity orientation

Roughly 95% of the endowment is invested in assets expected to produce equity-like returns. Fixed income and cash — which preserve capital but do not grow it — are deliberately minimised. This reflects Yale’s perpetual time horizon: when you invest forever, you can afford to ride out volatility in exchange for higher long-run returns.

2. Radical diversification through alternatives

Unlike a traditional 60/40 portfolio, Yale allocates heavily to alternative asset classes: private equity, venture capital, hedge funds (absolute return strategies), real assets, and natural resources. These alternatives historically offer returns uncorrelated with public markets, improving the portfolio’s risk-adjusted performance.

3. Illiquidity as an opportunity

Yale deliberately accepts illiquidity. Private equity and venture capital investments are locked up for 7–12 years. Yale can afford this because, unlike an individual investor, it never needs to liquidate everything at once. For a family with a long time horizon, accepting illiquidity in part of the portfolio is a powerful return enhancer.

4. Manager selection as the primary edge

Yale does not pick stocks — it picks managers. Identifying and accessing top-quartile private equity, venture capital, and hedge fund managers is where Yale believes its true edge lies. This is manager alpha, not market alpha.

What heirs can apply from the Yale Model

The Yale Model is not directly replicable for most family wealth holders — Yale has access to top-quartile managers who will not accept smaller investors. However, its principles translate:

  • Extend your time horizon and accept some illiquidity for the return premium it offers
  • Think in decades, not quarters — do not let short-term noise force long-term mistakes
  • Diversify aggressively across asset classes, not just within equities
  • Invest through relationships and due diligence, not product shelves
The 2024 caveat

Yale’s 5.7% return in FY2024 lagged the S&P 500’s ~25% gain significantly. CIO Mendelsohn acknowledged this directly: heavy private asset allocation lags in strong public market years when exits are limited. This is the cost of the Yale model in the short term — and why the model only works for investors with genuine long-term conviction. FY2025 recovered strongly at 11.1%.

The UBS Family Office Benchmark

How do the world’s largest family offices actually allocate capital? The UBS Global Family Office Report — covering 317 offices with an average net worth of $2.7 billion — is the closest thing to a peer benchmark for ultra-high-net-worth investing.

What $2.7 Billion Families Actually Do

The UBS Global Family Office Report 2025 surveys 317 single family offices across 30+ markets, managing portfolios averaging $1.1 billion with families averaging $2.7 billion in net worth. It is the most comprehensive annual snapshot of how the ultra-wealthy invest.

What APAC family offices do differently

For your context as an heir in Southeast Asia, the APAC-specific findings from UBS 2024 are particularly relevant:

  • APAC family offices hold more alternatives than the global average
  • 45% plan to increase developed market fixed income exposure
  • 31% plan to increase hedge fund allocations over 5 years
  • 36% are focused on impact investing — significantly above global peers
  • Healthcare is the top thematic (59%) followed by clean tech (40%) and philanthropy (39%)
  • 78% of APAC family office portfolios are actively managed, versus 64% globally
The concentration problem

The UBS data also highlights a critical risk for families of your type: only 53% of family offices globally have a formal succession plan in place. And only 26% involve the next generation from the outset. The most common reason for not having a plan: “We have time.”

For a newly inheriting heir, this data underscores the urgency of engagement — not passivity.

What this benchmark means for you
  • A 40%+ allocation to alternatives is appropriate if you have a long time horizon and can tolerate illiquidity
  • Impact and ESG integration is not a niche preference in APAC — it is mainstream in family office circles
  • Cash above 10% of the portfolio is an opportunity cost, not safety — especially in moderate-inflation environments
  • Your private banker’s standard product shelf does not look like this portfolio — most of the value is in private markets that require active sourcing

Understanding Risk: Not Just Volatility

Your private banker will manage volatility. Nobody else will manage your liquidity risk, concentration risk, estate tax risk, or the risk that your structures fail in a jurisdiction you didn’t anticipate. These are the risks that actually destroy inherited wealth.

The Risks That Don’t Show Up on a Bloomberg Screen

Investment risk is typically measured by volatility — the standard deviation of returns. But for an inheriting heir, volatility is often the least of your problems. Here are the risks that matter more.

1. Concentration risk

Many inherited estates arrive heavily concentrated in a single asset: the family business, one building, one stock position. A 70% concentration in any single asset is an existential risk. Diversification is not optional — it is the foundation of wealth preservation.

The challenge: selling concentrated positions creates capital gains tax events. The solution: plan the diversification strategically over time, using charitable structures, options overlays, or exchange funds where available.

2. Liquidity risk

An estate tax bill or unexpected family expense can force the sale of illiquid assets at exactly the wrong moment. Always maintain a liquidity reserve — typically 5–10% of the portfolio in cash or near-cash instruments accessible within 30 days.

For family offices with high alternative allocations, liquidity risk is structural: 20–30% of the portfolio might be locked in private equity funds with 10-year horizons. Model your liquidity carefully.

3. Sequence of returns risk

If you are drawing income from your portfolio — for living expenses, philanthropy, or family distributions — the order of returns matters enormously. A 30% drawdown in year 1 of distributions can permanently impair a portfolio even if long-run average returns are fine. This is why family offices set spending rates (typically 4–5% per year) and hold liquidity buffers.

4. Currency risk

A Singapore-based family with property in Bangkok, shares in New York, and cash in Manila faces five-way currency exposure. Unexpected currency moves can erode returns even when underlying assets perform well. Know your currency exposures and decide deliberately which to hedge.

5. Governance risk

This is the risk nobody talks about in investment presentations: that poor family decision-making, conflict, or lack of accountability erodes wealth faster than any market downturn. A 20-year study of 3,200 families by The Williams Group found that 85% of wealth transfer failures were attributed to poor trust and communication — not financial mismanagement.

Governance structures — family constitutions, investment committees with clear mandates, agreed spending policies — are not bureaucracy. They are risk management.

6. The risk of doing nothing

Cash left uninvested in a 3% inflation environment loses 3% of its real purchasing power per year. Over a decade, $10 million in cash becomes worth roughly $7.4 million in real terms. The greatest risk for a newly inheriting heir is often paralysis — deferring decisions until the “right time” that never arrives.

Impact & Gender-Lens Investing

Impact investing is not charity. The world’s leading family offices are allocating billions to investments that target both financial returns and measurable positive outcomes — and APAC is ahead of the global curve.

Aligning Wealth with Values — Without Sacrificing Returns

A common misconception: impact investing requires accepting below-market returns. The evidence increasingly suggests otherwise. A 2023 meta-analysis of over 2,000 impact studies by the Global Impact Investing Network (GIIN) found that the majority of impact investments met or exceeded investor return expectations.

The spectrum of impact

Not all impact investing is the same. The spectrum runs from:

  • ESG Integration: incorporating environmental, social, and governance factors into standard investment analysis to identify risks and opportunities. No explicit impact target — purely risk-management oriented.
  • Negative Screening: excluding sectors or companies that conflict with your values (weapons, tobacco, fossil fuels). The simplest form of values alignment.
  • Best-in-class ESG: investing in the top ESG performers within each sector rather than excluding entire sectors.
  • Thematic Investing: targeting specific themes — clean energy, financial inclusion, gender equity — with dedicated capital.
  • Impact-First Investing: explicitly targeting measurable positive outcomes alongside financial returns, with impact metrics as important as financial metrics.
  • Philanthropy: capital deployed for maximum impact with no expectation of financial return.

Most family office portfolios combine several of these approaches.

Gender-lens investing

Gender-lens investing (GLI) considers gender as an investment factor — either investing in companies that advance gender equality, or applying gender analysis to all investment decisions. Relevant lenses include:

  • Companies with gender-diverse leadership (women on boards and in executive roles)
  • Products and services that improve outcomes for women and girls
  • Financial inclusion investments that extend capital to women-owned businesses in emerging markets
  • Venture capital specifically targeting female founders (who receive only ~2% of global VC despite outperforming on a risk-adjusted basis)

For a next-generation female heir leading family wealth in Southeast Asia, GLI is both values-aligned and commercially interesting. APAC family offices report 36% engagement with impact investing — well above the 26% global average (UBS 2024).

Practical entry points
  • Public markets: gender-diversity ETFs (e.g. MSCI Women’s Leadership Index), ESG-screened equity funds
  • Private equity: impact PE funds focused on Southeast Asian financial inclusion, healthcare, or education
  • Direct investing: co-investments in companies explicitly addressing gender or climate challenges
  • Blended finance: de-risked structures that combine philanthropic and commercial capital for development outcomes
The measurement question

The key discipline in impact investing is measurement. Establish upfront what outcomes you are targeting (e.g. tonnes of CO₂ avoided, number of women accessing credit) and how you will measure them. The IRIS+ framework from GIIN provides standardised impact metrics used across the industry.

Generating Returns: How Portfolios Actually Make Money

“How do you generate alpha?” was the question your classmate asked and most bankers can’t properly answer. Here are the four actual mechanisms by which a well-managed portfolio generates returns above the market.

Beyond “We Outperform the Market”

When Leo asked “how do you generate alpha?” at your class presentation, he was asking the right question. Here is a clear-eyed answer.

Return Source 1: Market Beta

Simply being invested in a rising market produces returns. A diversified global equity portfolio in a growth year returns 15–25% with no skill required — just beta. This is the baseline, not the achievement. Any return claim must be measured against the relevant beta exposure first.

Return Source 2: Factor Premiums

Academic research has identified several systematic factors that produce returns above simple market beta over the long run:

  • Value: cheap stocks (low P/E, P/B) outperform expensive ones over time — the “value premium”
  • Size: small-cap stocks outperform large-caps — the “size premium”
  • Momentum: recent outperformers continue outperforming in the short term
  • Quality: companies with high profitability and low debt outperform
  • Low Volatility: paradoxically, lower-risk stocks have historically outperformed higher-risk ones on a risk-adjusted basis

These factor premiums are real but cyclical — they disappear for years at a time. A skilled manager captures them systematically. A mediocre manager confuses factor exposure for stock-picking skill.

Return Source 3: Illiquidity Premium

Investors who accept that they cannot sell an asset for 7–10 years demand a higher return than investors in liquid markets. This “illiquidity premium” is one of the most consistent sources of excess return available to wealthy families — who, unlike institutional investors constrained by daily liquidity, can actually afford to hold illiquid assets.

Private equity has historically delivered 3–5% per annum above public equity on a like-for-like basis, largely explained by this illiquidity premium plus the leverage and operational improvement that PE firms apply.

Return Source 4: Manager Skill (True Alpha)

True alpha — return generated by genuine insight, information, or execution advantage — is rare and difficult to sustain. The most consistent alpha generators are typically:

  • Top-quartile private equity managers with proprietary deal flow
  • Quantitative hedge funds with genuine data or model edges
  • Specialist sector investors with deep domain expertise
  • Venture capitalists with access to early-stage companies before they are widely discovered

For most public equity managers, what looks like alpha is usually factor exposure, luck, or survivorship bias. Over 10-year periods, fewer than 20% of active managers consistently outperform.

Putting it together

A sophisticated family office portfolio generates returns from all four sources — deliberately:

  1. Strategic beta exposure through low-cost index funds in efficient markets
  2. Factor tilts (value, quality) in public markets at modest additional cost
  3. Illiquidity premium through private equity, private credit, and real assets
  4. True alpha through careful selection of genuinely skilled managers in PE, VC, and specialised hedge funds

The goal is not to pick the best stock. It is to construct the right combination of return sources for your specific time horizon, risk tolerance, and liquidity needs.
